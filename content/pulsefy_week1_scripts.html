🎬 PART 2 — Production & Editing Playbook

Your master scene

You have ONE finished studio scene (anchor bottom-left and clear of the screen; Durban coastline window; rich DURBAN/KZN screen with events + map pins; Zulu shield on the desk). Save it as pulsefy_master_scene.png and reuse it every episode. You never re-position the anchor again — that job is done.

The layer model (CapCut)

Every episode is a stack of layers; only the screen-content layer changes week to week:

  • Layer 1 — Background: the master scene (reused).
  • Layer 2 — Screen content: this week's flyer / map recording / B-roll, sized to sit inside the screen area. The only layer you swap.
  • Layer 3 — Talking head: short lip-sync clips for intro + outro only.
  • Layer 4 — Segment label: small SPOTLIGHT / MAP DROP tag (Canva).
  • Layer 5 — Captions: CapCut auto-captions.
  • Layer 6 — Audio: voiceover (voice-changed) + music + news sting.

Per-episode edits (~6, most take seconds)

🎭 Talking Anchor — Lips & Motion

Honest truth: lips are easy, hands are hard. Free tools lip-sync a still image well, but none reliably animate gesturing hands — don't let hands hold up your launch.

The trick: only animate the 3–5 seconds he's actually on camera (intro line + outro CTA). The middle is screen content + B-roll + voiceover with the anchor NOT in frame. Cuts the work ~80%.

🎙️ Voiceover + AI Voice Change

You speak the lines yourself, then change your voice so the anchor sounds like a different presenter. No extra app — CapCut has a built-in Voice Changer.

  • Record your voice in CapCut (Audio → Record) or import it.
  • Select the clip → tap Voice changer → pick an effect until it sounds like a different presenter.
  • Split out the intro + outro audio — those feed Hedra for lip-sync.
  • Upgrade later: ElevenLabs free tier has a realistic speech-to-speech voice changer.

🎤 PART 3 — Meet Charlie, the Host

Charlie is the face of the desk. Lock his character so the satire is consistent every episode — same voice, same cheek, same warmth. Every script line should sound like it came out of his mouth.

Who he is

  • Charlie — mid-20s, born-and-bred Durban. Sharp, quick-witted, effortlessly charming.
  • A smart young man who reads the city like a local: taxi routes, braai spots, group-chat debates.
  • Credible enough to "report the news," cheeky enough to wink at the camera.
  • Confident but self-aware — he'll happily make Pulsefy (and himself) the butt of the joke.

His comedic voice

  • Deadpan news delivery of slightly absurd observations ("Breaking: man caught turning meat that wasn't his").
  • Mock-serious "breaking news" energy applied to nightlife and shisanyama.
  • Local, specific references: taxis, load-shedding, Sunday plans, outfit panic, group chats.
  • Warm sarcasm — he teases the situation, never insults a person or venue.
  • Lands the punchline, then a beat — a smirk to camera does half the work.
Charlie-isms — his signature lines / flavour 
OPEN:       "Good evening Durban - you're watching the Pulsefy News Desk."
SIGN-OFF:   "That's tonight's pulse."
FILLER:     "Here's the report..." / "Sources say..." / "This just in..." / "Consider it settled."
SELF-AWARE: "...we honestly lost count after the third lounge."
CTA:        "Want YOUR event or venue on this desk? It's free - link in bio."

Satire rules (keep it funny AND safe)

  • Punch at situations, never at people or a venue's quality.
  • When in doubt, make Charlie / Pulsefy the joke.
  • Keep it light, local and warm — clever, not cruel.
  • No politics, no punching down. Satire that makes people feel seen, not attacked.

Your Week 1 hooks already carry Charlie's voice — keep writing every future line as if he's saying it out loud with a smirk.
